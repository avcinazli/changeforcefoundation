Women in Tech: Stories Making a Difference in the Ecosystem

The gender gap in tech is real — and so are the women closing it, one career at a time.

Ask a room of ten tech workers how many are women, and in most countries the answer is three or fewer. The reasons are well documented: fewer girls steered towards technical subjects, hiring processes tuned to one kind of CV, workplaces where women are talked over, and career breaks penalized instead of understood. None of these reflect ability. All of them reflect design — and design can be changed.

Why the Salesforce ecosystem is a good battleground

Some corners of tech are easier to enter mid-career than others, and the Salesforce ecosystem is one of the friendliest. Configuration-based roles remove the "you must have coded since childhood" barrier. Certifications provide an objective, visible signal that cuts through biased CV screening. Remote and flexible work — common in the ecosystem — makes careers compatible with caregiving responsibilities that still fall disproportionately on women. And the community is unusually organized about the problem, with active groups dedicated to women's advancement in the ecosystem worldwide.

What actually helps

From our own programs, three things consistently make the difference for our women participants:

Role models who look like you. When your mentor is a woman who entered tech at 35 with two children, "people like me can do this" stops being a slogan and becomes a data point. This is why we match mentors and participants by shared experience where we can.

A community that normalizes questions. Many women arrive having been made to feel that asking a basic question confirms they don't belong. A cohort where everyone is learning — and where questions are the norm — dissolves that fear fast.

Concrete proof of skill. Certifications and real project experience are great levelers. A live system you configured yourself is hard to argue with in an interview.

The stories

In our community there are women who moved countries and rebuilt their careers from zero, mothers who studied after bedtime for months, and juniors who are now mentoring the next cohort. Each of their stories follows the same arc: someone opened a door, and they did the rest. We publish selected stories — with permission — in our Success Stories section.
